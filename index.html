De-escalating Agitated People

Equipping churches to be safe and welcoming spaces for all.

Welcome and Purpose

This training outlines principles and techniques to compassionately and safely de-escalate situations with individuals who may be disruptive due to distress, intoxication, or mental health challenges. Our goal is to maintain a peaceful environment while showing Christ's love to those in need.

Core Principles of De-escalation

Stay Calm (Yourself)

Your calm presence is contagious. Speak slowly and in a low tone. Control your breathing and non-verbal cues (e.g., uncross your arms).

Respect Personal Space

Stand at least 1.5 metres away. A non-threatening, hands-visible stance is best. Don't block their exit route.

Listen Actively - Validate Emotion

Allow them to vent. Validate their emotions ("I see that has made you feel angry") without necessarily agreeing with their actions.

Show Empathy & help to find options

Try to understand their perspective & help to find options "Help me understand what's happening" Use a compassionate and non-judgmental tone. Be sure to allow choices.

Key Takeaways

  • Prepare:

    Your body language is the first thing people notice. Take a deep breath and calm yourself. Try to be as relaxed as possible. Maintain a calm, open stance (no crossed arms), keep your hands visible, and respect personal space. Your facial expression should be neutral and compassionate, not judgmental. Use a low, calm tone of voice. Speak slowly and evenly. The volume and speed of your speech can either escalate or de-escalate the tension.

  • Validate Emotion:

    Don't interrupt. Let the person vent their frustrations. Reflect their feelings back to them (e.g., "It sounds like you're feeling ignored"). This validates their emotion and shows you are genuinely trying to understand. Do this in your own natural way. Continue until they can see that you accept their emotional state and are not judging them for it. "Until they feel validated."

  • Help them find options:

    It is important that an agitated person doesn't feel backed into a corner. Be sure to help them explore their choices. As they calm a little you might be able to help them with some options. e.g. "You could go in and shout at the vicar in front of everyone, but someone would probably call the police, or you could meet with the vicar after the service and talk about your concerns then.

  • Allow for choice:

    This empowers the agitated person to regain control. Offer positive choices. You might suggest... "Would it be helpful to talk with the vicar after this service, or perhaps we could make an appointment for you to raise your concerns later in the week when you are feeling a bit calmer?"

When De-escalation Fails

If the situation escalates or you feel unsafe, your safety is the priority. Follow these steps:

  1. Signal for Help: Discreetly get the attention of another team member or leader.
  2. Create Distance: Increase the space between you and the individual.
  3. Isolate (if possible): Guide others away from the immediate area.
  4. Do Not Hesitate to Call 111: If there is any threat of violence or immediate danger, call emergency services. Clearly state your location and the nature of the situation.
